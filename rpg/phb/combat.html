Combat

Rounds

In combat, the action happens in a series of rounds, each representing roughly six seconds of in-game time. During each round, each combatant gets to take a single turn, and possibly a single reaction. Within a round, turns are resolved in the logical order. If the order of action matters and it is not clear who should act first, the DM may call for an Initiative Check. This is usually a Dexterity check, but not always: you might roll Intelligence if casting a wizard spell, or Charisma for social interaction, for instance.

On your turn, you can generally take one standard action, one move action, and one swift action. You can exchange your standard action for an extra move action, or either or both of these for extra swift actions, but you cannot “trade up” in the other direction.

A standard action is your “main action” for the turn—usually something like attacking with a weapon or casting a spell. On a normal move action, you can walk a distance up to your Speed. This can be modified by special conditions like navigating difficult terrain, or can include special types of movement like climbing a rough surface or standing up from a prone position. (Most move actions involve movement, but not all of them do; this is a briefer name for what 3e calls “move-equivalent actions”.) Swift actions are quick, minor acts, like drawing or sheathing a weapon, casting a Quickened spell, etc.

Some actions take so little time or effort that they are classified as free actions. Common examples include dropping a held item, dropping to the floor, or speaking a few simple sentences. You can take as many free actions on a turn as the DM deems plausible.

Reactions are actions you can take in response to something, even if it isn’t your turn. The most common case of this is making an attack of opportunity against an enemy who has let down their defenses in some way. Again, you can normally take only a single reaction per round.

Behind the Curtain

I initially used a system of Fast and Slow Turns, but I’ve since moved to a more purely narrative initiative. For further discussion, see not only those particular sections, but that document overall.

Attacks

When you attack an NPC, you roll a d20 and add your Attack bonus. If this meets or exceeds the target’s Defense, the attack hits, and you roll damage. When an NPC attacks you, you roll a d20 and add your Defense bonus; if the result is below the attacker’s Attack score, your attempt at defense fails, and the attack is successful.

To represent the unpredictable nature of combat, if the die itself shows a 20 (before modification, i.e., a “natural 20”), an additional +10 bonus is applied to the result. If a natural 1 is rolled, a –10 penalty is imposed.

Behind the Curtain

Players rolling for their defense in addition to their attacks helps them stay more engaged, shifts the implied sense of agency, and can also help keep the game moving when monsters are attacking multiple PCs simultaneously. It also removes any possibility of the DM fudging dice, perhaps subconsciously ratcheting up the tension. Furthermore, it relieves the already overworked DM of a tiny amount of effort and distraction, allowing them to stay more focused on and engaged with the game and the players. (The gains are enhanced if the DM uses static [average] values for monster damage.)

This rule comes from the 3.5 Unearthed Arcana pp. 133–4, as well as the 5e Unearthed Arcana rules for Players Make All Rolls. This applies not only to attacks, but also to saving throws and spell resistance: see sources for details. However, the designers’ math is off: subtracting 10 from PCs’ static numbers (like AC or save DCs) to obtain the corresponding bonus is the most sensible thing to do, but then to preserve the probabilities of success and failure, you must add 12, not 11, to the NPCs’ bonuses for attacks and saves.

Some (but not all!) editions treated a natural 20 as an automatic hit and a natural 1 as an automatic miss. This is simple, but it sometimes leads to highly implausible results. Some modicum of sanity can be restored with little cost in extra complexity with the method given here, which is based on the system presented in the 3.0 DMG, p. 64 (tweaked slightly to fix the math). Additionally, it meshes nicely with my revised rules for critical hits.

Critical Hits

If an attack succeeds (or a defense fails) by more than 10, the attack is a critical hit, and additional damage is rolled for the hit. (This can stack: An attack that succeeds by more than 20 is a “double crit”, dealing yet another set of damage, for effectively triple the normal value; etc.)

Extra damage from a critical hit is handled like a separate hit with respect to choosing special effects in place of damage. So, if you land a critical hit on a normal attack, you might still deal normal damage, but in lieu of extra damage, you might opt for some special effect, subject to DM approval. Conversely, if you achieve a critical hit when attempting a special maneuver (say, to disarm your opponent), you might deal normal damage on top of that, or perhaps instead choose an additional effect (like a trip). Some of these may require you to negotiate some give-and-take with your DM.

Another option for a critical hit is to trade the extra damage for an extra attack instead, whether against the same target or another.

Behind the Curtain

Some editions treat a natural 20 on an attack roll as an automatic critical hit, but this sometimes stretches the bounds of credulity, especially if a 19 or better is required to hit the target in the first place. On the other hand, a highly skilled fighter with advantageous circumstances should be able to deal out loads of damage fairly consistently. 3e tried to remedy both of these situations with its system of threat ranges and damage multipliers, but this ballooned into quite a complex system, touching class features, feat lists, and weapon tables, among other things. The system presented here is based on comparing attack and defense values, and it should reward high combat skill (and/or good luck) with very little additional complexity.

Exceptions

Of course, everything is subject to the constraints of plausibility. An ooze, for example, is immune to critical hits, cannot be blinded or tripped, and is extra-difficult to push or grab. A ghost would be immune to nearly all of these special effects. If uncertain, check with your DM before committing to a course of action that depends upon special outcomes.
