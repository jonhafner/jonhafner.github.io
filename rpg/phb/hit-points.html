Hit Points

Hit points are an abstract measure of a creature’s “staying power” in combat. Physical health and hardiness obviously play a role, but so do combat skill, willpower, luck, divine grace, magical protections, and countless other factors.

Maximum Hit Points

The size of your hit point pool is determined by your Constitution score, your physical size, and your experience level.

Every living creature begins with a base hit point maximum equal to its Constitution score, scaled by size, as noted in the following table. So, for example, a human with a Constitution score of 13 begins with 13 hit points; as a Small creature, a hobbit with the same Constitution starts with 9 hp (13 × 3/4, rounded down).

Fine×1/8
Diminutive×1/4
Tiny×1/2
Small×3/4
Medium×1
Large×4/3
Huge×2
Gargantuan×4
Colossal×8

In addition, each level you have in a class supplies a Hit Die of a type specified in the class description. When you gain a level, roll the indicated die and add your Constitution modifier (minimum 1); add this to your hit point maximum. (Obviously, at character creation, do this for each level you begin with.) Hit points from levels are not scaled by size—the scaling applies only to the “base” hit points from your raw Constitution score.

When rolling a hit die to determine your hit points for that level, you may reroll a result you dislike, but at a –1 penalty on the reroll (minimum 1). You can repeat this as desired, but the penalty is cumulative. (This applies only to elite characters, such as player characters using 4d6 or an elite point buy for ability scores. Non-elite characters take their hit dice as rolled.)

Behind the Curtain

Aside from a couple changes to scaling based on creature size, this is essentially the Vitality and Wound Points system from 3.5 Unearthed Arcana pp. 115–119, except with wound and vitality points lumped into a single pool. This also nudges toward the greater survivability of characters in 4e, though not nearly to the extent seen in that edition. This inflation of hit points is partially counterbalanced by the much slower natural healing of classic editions.

The choice to add scaling factors for Small and Large creatures was made for several reasons (not least of which being the many advantages of Small size); the only question was whether to use 3/4 and 4/3 or 2/3 and 3/2. For average scores (10 or 11), the former yields effective adjustments of ±3 hp, which feels about right. (When I was considering static adjustments, I was leaning toward ±2, so that’s close enough.) However, the latter would produce –4 hp for Small and +5 hp for Large; that feels like it’s pushing it. Hence, my choice of the former.

The system of rerolling low results on hit dice exists to reduce the chances of being stuck with a punishingly low hit point total after several levels of bad luck, while still retaining some amount of risk and the associated excitement.

For DMs: Note that this significantly increases the hit point totals of most creatures unless their HD/levels are reduced accordingly. (Under this system, many baseline creatures should have no levels or Hit Dice at all, instead drawing upon their ability scores and size.)

Damage

Your maximum hit points measure how many points of damage you can suffer before being removed from combat. There are two common ways of tracking this: One is to subtract damage taken from your “current hit points”, with increasing concern as your “remaining” health approaches 0. The other is to total up how much damage has been dealt to you, with worry creeping in as that total approaches your hit point maximum. These two approaches are mathematically equivalent, and each has its advantages and disadvantages in different situations; use whichever you feel more comfortable with.

When you accumulate damage equaling or exceeding your hit points, you are removed from active participation in combat. The precise consequences of this depend upon circumstances: you might be knocked unconscious, still conscious but incapacitated, gravely wounded and in danger of dying, and so on, all depending on the source of the damage, the intent of the attacker, etc. If you do survive, temporary (or permanent!) ability score damage is not uncommon.

Damage taken in excess of your hit points carries over to be applied as ability score damage instead. The ability score affected may be determined by circumstances, but typically defaults to Constitution for normal damage. If your Constitution score reaches 0, you die.

Behind the Curtain

Most of this is fairly standard from the classic editions, with just a bit of additional abstraction and flexibility inserted. For some ideas for creative things that can be done with incapacitation, see the optional rules for lasting and permanent damage on p. 114 of the Numenera core book.

Healing

When you heal some amount of damage, whether this healing is from natural or magical causes, you recover the specified number of hit points, not to exceed your maximum. (Equivalently, the amount of damage you currently suffer cannot drop below zero.)

A common source of natural healing is your Hit Dice, if you have levels in a class (as most player characters do). As noted above, your Hit Dice contribute to your maximum hit point total. They also provide a healing resource. So, e.g., a third-level fighter has a pool of 3d10 HD upon which to draw. No more than once per hour, you can “take a breather” to spend one hit die: rest for a few minutes, mark off one die from your pool, roll that die, and heal that many hit points. You can also spend as many hit dice as you want (and have remaining) during or after your eight-hour nightly rest.

After a long rest, you naturally heal 1 hp and regain 1 spent HD, up to your maximum of each. (With bed rest and/or medical care, you may heal at a faster rate.)

Behind the Curtain

The mechanics here are clearly a simplified, restricted version of 5e’s Hit Dice, which in turn evolved from 4e’s Healing Surges. Systems like this do not significantly increase the durability of a party within a single encounter, but they do allow them to extend their adventuring day, even without access to significant quantities of healing magic. Keeping the recovery rates down, however, still retains some longer-term cost to taking significant damage. The problem of attrition is substantially lessened compared to early editions, but not entirely eliminated, unlike standard 4e and 5e in which a party is back up to full capacity after a single night’s rest.

Temporary Hit Points

Some effects can grant temporary hit points. These are effectively a separate pool of health that absorbs damage taken first. When you take damage, subtract it from your current total of temporary hit points remaining. When those are exhausted, any surplus damage is dealt to your character as normal.

Temporary hit points do not “stack”: if some effect grants you temporary hit points while you have some already, you must choose whether to keep your current total or to discard them and accept the new ones in their place. You may not combine them or otherwise retain both amounts.
