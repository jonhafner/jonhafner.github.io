Hit Die: d6; Base Attack Bonus: average; Good Saving Throws: Reflex

Combat Proficiencies: Rogues are proficient with all simple weapons, plus the hand crossbow, rapier, sap, shortbow, and short short sword. Rogues are proficient with light armor, but not with shields.

Bonus Skills: Rogues receive 4 bonus skill points per level (16 at first level).

Sneak Attack: If you can catch an opponent when they are unable to defend themselves, you can strike a vital spot for extra damage. Once per round, you get an automatic critical when you hit a creature that is flat-footed to your attack with a Finesse melee weapon or a ranged weapon. (Common ways to catch a target flat-footed are to attack from hiding or from behind, or when the target is surprised, balancing, or climbing.)

At first level, a sneak attack acts as an ordinary critical. At 5th level and every four levels thereafter, it increases in power: a double-crit, a triple-crit, etc. (See the Rogue class table above.) These are additive with an attack that is already a critical hit. (Note: Critical hits from sneak attack must be used to deal damage or other effects against that target; they cannot be converted into extra attacks.)

In order to strike with deadly accuracy, you must be able to see the target well enough to pick out a vital spot, and you must be able to reach such a spot. You cannot sneak attack while striking a creature with concealment or striking the limbs of a creature whose vitals are beyond reach. If attacking with a ranged weapon, your target must be within 30 feet.

Note that some creatures are immune to critical hits, and thus are unaffected by your sneak attack. Incorporeal creatures like ghosts and wraiths have no physical form, and oozes and jellies have no discernable anatomies. Even some creatures with solid form might still lack vulnerable areas, such as certain kinds of plants, constructs, or undead. Check with your DM if you are uncertain whether you can find a weak spot in your target.

Thieves’ Cant: Through your experience as a rogue you learned thieves’ cant, a secret mix of dialect, jargon, and code that allows you to hide messages in seemingly normal conversation. It is not a distinct language, but rather consists of slang words and implied meanings that can be worked into nearly any language; two thieves cannot communicate via thieves’ cant unless they know a common language. The vocabulary of thieves’ cant limits its use to discussing things that interest thieves: stolen loot, easy marks, breaking and entering, mugging, confidence games, and the like. The cant is also useful for identifying fellow cads and bounders by slipping a few tidbits of lingo into a normal conversation.

In addition, you understand a set of secret signs and symbols used to convey short, simple messages, such as whether an area is dangerous or the territory of a thieves’ guild, whether loot is nearby, or whether the people in an area are easy marks or will provide a safe house for thieves on the run.

Behind the Curtain

Regarding the class name: For quite some time, I was calling this class “Thief”. That might be wonderfully old-school, but it’s also potentially problematic, as it encourages players to steal from their fellow party members. (The only class that's worse in that regard is “assassin”.) “Rogue” is a much better name, supporting a broader range of archetypes, like scouts and spies. In my pregens, I used archetype names like “burglar” and “street ruffian” to emphasize their skill sets without overtly encouraging intra-party conflict.

Granting only 4 bonus skill points per level works because of my simplified skill system, with skills like Stealth, Security, and Legerdemain together covering most applications of Hide, Move Silently, Listen, Spot, Search, Disable Device, Open Locks, Pick Pockets, etc. A total of 6 points per level then is plenty, and even allows a background skill or two.

Much of the language around Sneak Attack comes from 3e and 5e, but in application, its mechanical effects are now much closer to 2e [and earlier?].

Thieves’ cant, as presented here, is a combination of its 2e and 5e versions—strangely absent from 3e!
