Differences from Third Edition

The “official” version of D&D that my ruleset most closely resembles is the Third Edition of the game (specifically, 3.5), so if you’re already familiar with that (or at least have a PHB on hand for reference), you can imagine starting with it and making the changes listed below.

General mood and visual aesthetic

Third Edition is sometimes credited with popularizing the “dungeonpunk” aesthetic, which can be fine if done intentionally (e.g., Eberron), but I’m not keen on it being the default. My first D&D book was the Second Edition PHB, in which the first full-color interior illustration was a particular piece by Larry Elmore. This went a long way toward setting the mood of the game for me. It might look a little old-fashioned now, but this is an example of the sort of thing I still think of when I imagine a typical world of Dungeons & Dragons—something that, despite its fantastical elements, is a bit more realistic and grounded than one in which it seems like every other fighter wears armor with spikes as big as their head and carries a sword as long as they are tall. So, in short, the illustrations in the Third Edition books are sometimes useful, but should not be taken as accurate representations of the game world.

Unfortunately, most of the art from the Second Edition era was not up to the same level of quality as the painting cited above. Thankfully, though, the most extreme excesses seem to have moderated considerably over the course of the next couple of editions, to the point where most Fifth Edition artwork is much more reasonable, albeit still somewhat stylized. (Even the more fantastical examples are usually nice to look at, so I recommend them for inspiration.) Feel free to use most of Fifth Edition as a resource, checking with me as needed if you’re unsure if something fits my campaign.

Ability Scores

In the interest of maintaining balanced power levels between PCs, instead of the semi-random method presented in the PHB, we’ll use a more deterministic approach as presented in the DMG.

For a more authentically “old-school” experience, consider first drafting a “target” set of scores by using the method described in the PHB (roll 4d6, drop the lowest, six times), but record the scores in order, assigning the first to Strength, the second to Dexterity, etc. Then use an array or point buy system to try to get as close as possible to that score spread. Behind the Curtain

The Standard Point Buy generally yields ability score sets that are fairly typical of those you would expect from the 4d6–L method. (I once did a very detailed analysis and independently arrived at a point budget and scale that were only very slightly different from what’s given in the DMG—close enough that I didn’t see the point in changing it.)

For non-elite characters (whether PCs in a gritty campaign, or ordinary NPCs), either use the Average Array of 13, 12, 11, 10, 9, and 8, or apply the point-buy with only 15 points. This produces results closer to what might be expected from the original 3d6 method.

Abilities and Spellcasters

Bonus spell slots for high ability scores (PHB pg. 8, Table 1-1) are moved back by a single point, and also extended to include minor spells (cantrips and orisons, called 0-level in 3e). In other words, a score of 11 provides a bonus minor spell; at 13, also a bonus Level I spell; etc. To wit:

Behind the Curtain

Regarding bonus minor spells, this is one of many places where I simply extend existing patterns in the natural fashion: why don’t more talented spellcasters get more cantrips/orisons?

As for pushing everything back one step, this is one of a few areas where it’s easy to make odd-numbered ability scores more significant, in response to the dominance of even-numbered scores for the ability score modifiers. (Standard DCs and unstressed checks are another place where I feel I’ve made progress on this.)

The Abilities

Many specific applications (most notably the business about half your Strength bonus to off-hand attacks and 1.5× to two-handed attacks, as well as the assorted skills) are likely to change during the process of simplifying rules. Focus on general concepts, not specifics.

Changing Ability Scores

The stated increase to an ability score at every fourth level is not necessarily automatic, depending on the campaign; check with your DM.

Chapter 2: Races

As in the previous chapter, many references to specific skill checks will change due to the changes to the skill system (see below). Focus on general concepts, not specific skills.

Racial Characteristics

Favored Class

Ignore this section, as well as any future reference to favored classes. The Favored Class rules are an unnecessary complication to the multiclassing system.

Humans

Feats are no longer in use (see below). Instead, if you play a human, you choose any two ability scores, and add +1 to each. (This applies only to “elite” humans, but in most campaigns, that includes player characters.)

Dwarves

Observe that several of the listed racial traits can be condensed to a +2 bonus to any ability/skill checks related to stone or metal.

Gnomes

Gnomes are complicated, and I still haven't worked out how to resolve the differences between forest gnomes, rock gnomes, and tinker gnomes. Please try to avoid them for now, but if they really speak to you, check with me before doing any significant amount of work with them.

Halflings

Halflings will require a serious revision to flavor, but their mechanics are probably mostly fine, and can work as-is for now. If interested, perhaps talk with me so we can discuss their “fluff”.

Chapter 3: Classes

Class and Level Bonuses

A minor point, but note that the lower save bonus isn’t truly “poor”, but merely average. (For any given save, the majority of classes have the lower bonus progression. This effect is even stronger if one includes the NPC classes from the DMG.)

Regarding the base attack bonuses, ignore the system of iterative attacks at lower bonuses. Multiple attacks per round will be handled by other means. Only pay attention to the first number, not those after slashes.

Level-Dependent Benefits

The precise XP chart is likely to change, in keeping with the exponential progression of earlier editions.

Also, no distinction will be made between class skills and cross-class skills: the Class Skill Max Ranks apply to all skills, regardless of your class. Furthermore, recall that feats and ability score increases are no longer part of the system in the form presented here. In other words, the only useful part of Table 3–2 is the maximum skill ranks, which can be summarized as simply character level + 3.

Class Descriptions

Class Skills: Again, no game-mechanical distinction is drawn between class skills and cross-class skills. When reading individual class entries, treat this item as merely a list of suggested or recommended skills.

Barbarian, Bard, Druid, Monk, Paladin, Ranger, Sorcerer

I have not yet reviewed these classes; if interested, check with me first.

Cleric

Clerics are now spontaneous spellcasters. Furthermore, some adjustments will likely be made to their ability to channel energy (such as to turn or rebuke undead). Stay tuned.

Fighter

Saving Throws: A fighter may choose whether to have their “good” save in Fortitude or Reflex.

Bonus Feats: As noted elsewhere, feats per se are being eliminated. Some of them will be replaced with a system of combat proficiencies, of which fighters will receive the most slots.

Furthermore, a fighter will receive one additional attack per round at 5th level and every fourth level thereafter.

Rogue

Skill Points: Rogues receive bonus skill points. (+2 per level? +8 at 1st level?)

Sneak Attack: This feature will soon be folded into the critical hit mechanics and simplified.

Most other class abilities can either be dropped or incorporated into the system of combat proficiencies.

Wizard

Familiar: Not all arcanists have familiars; to allow some flexibility (and for other reasons), the binding of a familiar is moved to the purview of a spell, as in other editions.

Bonus Feats: These are no longer necessary. Metamagic feats will be incorporated into the spellcasting system, and item creation feats are no longer needed, as the ability to craft appropriate items is now part of the scope of any spellcasting class.

Multiclass Characters

As noted elsewhere, certain parts of the multiclassing system don’t work entirely correctly. If multiclassing, you must use the fractional bonus tables (to be provided soon).

Also, related to the irrelevance of favored classes, there are no XP penalties for multiclassing with uneven levels.

Chapter 4: Skills

While the basic approach to skills remains much the same at the highest level, skills are undergoing enough changes to warrant their own document.

Chapter 5: Feats

Feats, per se, no longer exist. Some were incorporated into class features, built into the core system, or adapted in other ways; others were rendered obsolete by other changes. The chief remnant is the system of combat proficiencies, which in many ways resembles the system of earlier editions, before it was swallowed up by feats.

Chapter 7: Equipment

Equipping a Character

If you don’t use a starting package for your class, instead purchase your equipment out of the average starting gold indicated. (Do not roll randomly.)

Weapons

The rules for weapons will be simplified somewhat in the future; for now, ignore the rules for critical hits (including the “Critical” column on the weapon table), as they are superceded by my own.

Armor

Arcane spell failure only applies to some casters, so will be dealt with in those class descriptions when they are written. For now, assume wizards cannot cast spells with somatic components while wearing armor, while sorcerers and warlocks have no such constraints, as their gestures are not so precise.

Behind the Curtain

This is a return to the conventions of classic editions. The attempt to codify arcane spell failure in the core system introduced needless complexity.

Chapter 8: Combat

Much of this material has already changed, and more is sure to come. For details, see my documents presenting some revised rules for combat and hit points. (These are in early draft stages currently, nowhere near complete; where PHB material is not contradicted, it likely still holds.) A few major differences:

  • Initiative system (if you can call it that) borrowed from Shadow of the Demon Lord.
  • In combat, players roll most of the dice, both to attack and defend.
  • The critical hit rules have been tremendously simplified.
  • A character’s raw Constitution score is included in hit point calculations. This slightly increases the hit point totals of most baseline creatures, and more significantly increases the hit points of characters who have a modest number of class levels.
  • The rules for death and dying are almost entirely replaced with situational adjudication.
  • Saving throws are not subject to automatic failures and successes: a natural 1 and a natural 20 have their usual numerical values with no special effects.
  • With revisions to systems for multiple attacks, there is no longer a need for a full-round “Full Attack” action; the “Attack” standard action covers it.
  • For characters with class levels, many (or most) of their hit points are more abstract than physical—so damage is damage, whether it is intended to kill or subdue. There is no need to track nonlethal damage separately from the regular sort; the difference only matters when handling a character who has run out of hit points.
