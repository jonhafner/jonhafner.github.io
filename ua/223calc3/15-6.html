§ 15.6: Triple Integrals

Triple integrals are just like double integrals, except more so. I say that somewhat facetiously, but it’s pretty accurate, as most of what you’ve done with double integrals extends to triple integrals in very natural ways. The domains of integration are now three-dimensional regions in space, making the setup the hardest part of most of these. Pay close attention to the examples, and see my example if necessary: “§ 15.6: Integral over a tetrahedron” (YouTube | Panopto).

Some of you might initially have some trouble understanding what these integrals even mean. Unless you have a unique ability to visualize four-dimensional objects and their hypervolumes, it won’t be productive to think of the integrand as a length; this is where other interpretations, such as density or temperature, are generally more intuitive. If this is a sticking point for you, perhaps skip ahead to the subsection on “Applications of Triple Integrals” for several ways of interpreting them conceptually.

If you are becoming alarmed by the apparent trend of ever-increasing numbers of integrals, don’t worry! Yes, the theory does extend to ℝn, but unless you’re working in some sort of abstract state space, three or fewer integrals are enough to cover the vast majority of applications you’ll encounter—probably due to us inhabiting three spatial dimensions. Certainly, for what we’re doing in this class, we cap out at triple integrals.
