Calculus III

§ 13.3: Arc Length and Curvature

Arc Length

Recall from Calculus II that we had three (or four, depending on how you count) different formulae for the length of a curve. The details varied depending on how the curve was represented—y or x as a function of the other, x and y as functions of a parameter t, or r as a function of θ in polar—but they all had certain high-level structural similarities. We can compute the length of a curve C by the abstract formula L = ∫C ds, where

ds² = dx² + dy²

by the Pythagorean Theorem, and we perform appropriate manipulations to find an equation giving ds as a square root of some expression times some other differential. The parametric version of this is reproduced in this section, as formula 1. In three dimensions, the situation is similar, but of course we have

ds² = dx² + dy² + dz².

Factoring dt out of the RHS and solving for ds yields formula 2 in the text.

Note that vector notation allows us to express the parametric arc length formula for any number of dimensions in the compact form of formula 3. The note immediately preceding Example 1 ties this into what I mentioned in the previous section, that the tangent vector can be thought of as a velocity.

For an example of computing arc length, see “§ 13.3a: Arc length (Exercise #6)” (YouTube | Panopto).

The Arc Length Function

This can be thought of as simply the length along the curve from a fixed anchor point to an arbitrary, nonspecific point determined by t. Note that this contains a sense of direction, measuring in the direction of increasing t; moving in the opposite direction will produce a negative value. Compare to the arc length functions in § 8.1 and § 10.2.

For an example, including reparametrizing by arc length, see “§ 13.3b: Arc length function (Exercise #16)” (YouTube | Panopto).

Curvature

Note that the symbol used for curvature is the lowercase Greek letter kappa, not the Latin letter K.

Curvature is a scalar (not vector) value that measures the rate of change of direction of a curve, as radians per unit of arc length. A more geometric interpretation comes via Example 3.

Several expressions for curvature are stated in this subsection. For theoretical purposes, Definition 8 is primary, but in general, the most useful formula for computation is given by Theorem 10. It’s good to be aware of the others, as they can be handy from time to time, but for practical purposes they can be considered auxiliary.

For an example, see “§ 13.3c: Curvature (Exercise #32)” (YouTube | Panopto).
