§ 12.2: Vectors

In this context, we commonly define a vector as a quantity having both magnitude and direction. Aside from a single exception (the zero vector has no well-defined direction), this works well enough for our purposes.

The early portions of this section give some physical examples of vectors, such as displacement, velocity, acceleration, and force; momentum is another common example from physics. In their strict technical senses, all of these quantities specify not only magnitudes, but also directions.

Some of those quantities have special names for their magnitudes: the magnitude of a displacement is a distance, and the magnitude of a velocity is a speed. Distance and speed are simple real numbers, with no sense of direction; these are called scalars. Other common examples of scalars include length, area, volume, time, mass, energy, temperature … probably the majority of “normal” quantities, numbers, or variables you are accustomed to dealing with are scalars.

Geometric Description of Vectors

Read this subsection carefully. Note the importance of distinguishing between vectors and scalars in print and writing! It is difficult to write in “bold print” by hand, so additional marks are commonly used. For example, the vector v is perhaps most commonly written with a small arrow ( v⃗ ) or half-arrow ( v⃑ ) above. Some writers place these symbols below the variable instead, or place a bar or tilde above or below, though these are much less common. Whichever notation you choose, be sure to write it clearly and consistently!

The two most common geometric interpretations of vector addition are given by the Triangle Law and the Parallelogram Law, as illustrated in Figures 3 and 4. You will probably come to favor one over the other, but sometimes one is more natural than the other in a particular situation, so make sure you understand and can use both.
