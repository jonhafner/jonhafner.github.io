§ 16.5: Curl and Divergence

Curl

I remarked in my notes for the previous section that the expression Qx − Py describes the amount of counterclockwise rotation of F at each point in the xy-plane. Using the right-hand rule in a fashion similar to how we describe torque, we could take that value as a coefficient on k̂, i.e., a vector component in the z-direction. Analogous expressions can be developed for the other planes; combining these gives the expression in Definition 1 for describing the total “curl” of a three-dimensional vector field. This is difficult to memorize, so I suggest reading through the work that follows it to develop the shorthand “definition” in formula 2. That’s the form that I always use myself when computing curl, as in Example 1.

Read the rest of this subsection carefully. In the discussion of conservative vector fields, recall that for a two-dimensional field, we checked whether Qx = Py; observe that this is true if and only if Qx − Py = 0. Applying similar principles to the other components, we see that the conditions we get from Clairaut’s Theorem are equivalent to checking whether the curl equals the zero vector.

Divergence

Read this subsection carefully. Theorem 11 and the Laplace operator are good things to be aware of, but I don’t think you’ll be responsible for them on the exam. If they show up in exercises, just refer back to the text. The rest of the subsection is very important, however.

Vector Forms of Green’s Theorem

If we think of the gradient as a sort of “derivative” of a scalar field, then curl and divergence are two kinds of “derivatives” of vector fields. They both measure how a field changes, but in different ways: divergence measures how it expands or contracts, and curl measures how it rotates.

Both of these, as it turns out, relate to Green’s Theorem, as described in this subsection. This subsection is optional—you will not be responsible for its content. However, if you want to gain a bit more intuitive and geometric insight into Green’s Theorem, and to lay some groundwork for Stokes’ Theorem and the Divergence Theorem in later sections, this subsection is worth reading and thinking about.
