Discrete Math

§ 8.2: Reflexivity, Symmetry, and Transitivity

Suggested Reading

Entire section. Pay particular attention to Example 8.2.4; again, the concept of congruence will be important later (soon!).

Exercises

Do enough of #1–33 to be confident that you can work with anything of this type on a test. (If you don’t work through all of them, at least read them all to see more examples of the variety of types of relations.)

#34–42 are good things to think about; even if you lack the time, skill, or inclination to produce solid proofs, at least consider whether each statement is true or false and why.

#43–50 are interesting extensions of these ideas, but will not be tested.

#51–53 are on the topic of transitive closure. These are a bit tedious due to their size; if I ask any questions on this topic, they will require less work than this. (In particular, #53 is quite tedious to write out, but it should be fairly easy to see what the solution must be if you draw a diagram and think about reachability.) Do only as much as necessary for you to be confident in your abilities.

If you have any experience programming and want some short exercises to play with, consider #54–56. The text expects just a bit of pseudocode, but writing actual code in the language of your choice could be useful practice if you’re so inclined. (If you struggle with how to represent a relation as a data structure, you might consider an adjacency matrix. It looks like a decent introduction can be found in § 10.2: Matrix Representations of Graphs, in the subsection called “Matrices and Directed Graphs”.)
