Introduction

Read the section intro carefully. Do not start skimming because it reminds you of grade school: it’s important to make sure you really understand the decimal number system and the terminology associated with it. We’re going to build on this in our discussion of other number systems.

Across cultures, many number systems treat the number ten with some significance, probably because we happen to have ten fingers. Even cultures that didn’t use positional numeral systems were often built from tens or groups of tens; see Roman numerals and Greek numerals for two different methods in this vein.

However, systems built on other bases have also existed. The English terms dozen (12), gross (144 = 12²), and great gross (1728 = 12³) suggest a duodecimal or “dozenal” system. Some systems are based on twenties; Mayan numerals are the most famous example of this, but we also see remnants of such a system in French, and other examples abound. Even in English we have the word score, as in “Four score and seven years ago”. The ancient Babylonians used a base-60 system (though it sort of had a base-10 system embedded within it); the adoption of Babylonian methods by Greek astronomers is why we have 60 seconds in a minute, 60 minutes in an hour, and 360° in a full turn.

These are interesting historical curiosities, but of interest to us here are those number systems which are most useful for digital computers, and due to their yes/no, on/off, true/false nature, those of greatest application tend to have bases which are powers of 2. Most commonly, we are interested in base 2 (which we call binary) and base 16 (hexadecimal), though base 8 (octal) also sees some use, as do others. (Bases 32 and 64 are sometimes used to represent binary data as compactly as possible in plain ASCII, for instance.) So, this brings us to the first subsection:
