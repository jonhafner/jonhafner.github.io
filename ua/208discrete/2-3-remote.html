Additional Valid Argument Forms: Rules of Inference

Read through this portion of the text, thinking carefully about why each argument form is valid. If it’s not clear, a truth table may help; if you still don’t have an intuition for a particular rule after that, talk to me about it.

Some of these rules can be thought of as “utilities”, not seeming to do much on their own but useful as part of a longer chain of reasoning. (I feel like Generalization, Specialization, and Conjunction are good examples of this type.)

Others, though, more closely resemble what we might already think of as logical reasoning. Elimination, for instance, is associated with what is commonly called “the process of elimination”. Proof by Division into Cases is the formal structure of a proof technique we will apply to number theory in § 4.5, and the Contradiction Rule (introduced near the end of the section) will be useful to us starting in § 4.7.

Fallacies

Read this section carefully. Presently, the fallacies of greatest interest to us are converse and inverse error, which are examples of formal fallacies—it is the form or structure of the argument that is invalid, permitting false conclusions. In other settings, however, you are much more likely to encounter informal fallacies, where the problem is not the form but the content; these include using ambiguous premises or circular reasoning/begging the question.

Pay close attention to the concept of a sound argument, and how this differs from whether an argument is valid. (In particular, observe that if you see an argument with an obviously false conclusion, you know it’s not sound, but it may still be valid!)
