Entire section. In particular, the text has more thorough and rigorous explanations of the construction of adder circuits and the properties of two’s complement.

Exercises

Those that look like plausible test questions include #1–20, 23–36, 38–40 (except with smaller values to be faster without calculators), and 41–46. With the possible exception of #38–40, you should be able to do all the required arithmetic by hand; if you use a calculator at all, it should only be as a check. Notes:

  • In #17–20, feel free to either subtract directly (borrowing as needed) or add the two’s complement of the subtrahend, as you prefer.
  • Feel free to skip #37(b) unless you want to dig deeper into the theory behind two’s complement.
  • Side note on #47: Just as hexadecimal can be thought of as a shorthand for binary in four-bit chunks, octal translates binary in three-bit chunks. That might sound less useful—when do we care about precisely three bits?—but it is occasionally handy. One of the most common applications is probably the use of chmod and the like to set the read/write/execute permissions for files in Unix-like operating systems, with the digits 4–7 representing the most frequently used options on single-user systems.
