Calculus II

Expectations and advice

Expectations and preparation: We expect that you are fully capable with all material from the main sequence: arithmetic, algebra, geometry, trigonometry, and Calculus I. In particular, you will need to be able to quickly and accurately apply the standard techniques of differentiation and integration. You will need to be able to differentiate all the standard basic functions, as well as their products, quotients, and compositions. You will also need to be able to integrate common functions and apply basic integration techniques such as substitution. Make sure you review any topics you think might give you trouble, and ask me questions if you encounter difficulties.

Our textbook can obviously serve as a review of previous calculus material, but it also includes reviews of pre-calculus topics in Appendices A–D and Chapter 1. If you think you might be rusty on any of these topics, or have missed one or two points in your previous education, you might wish to give them a skim and watch out for anything you are less familiar with.

Advice: One of the best things you can do early on is to carefully read and consider Dr. Kreider’s advice for Being Prepared For Calculus. (It was written for his Calculus I class, but I feel it applies to most mathematics courses at the 100–300 level.)

The only thing I would add to that advice is some discussion of scheduling and pacing. You may have heard the rule of thumb that you can generally expect to spend two to three hours outside of class for every hour you spend in class. In our accelerated summer schedule, we have a little over six hours per week in class, so you can expect to need 12–18 hours per week for reading, homework, studying for tests, etc. (Some of you may be able to get away with less, but some of you may need more!) If you take one day a week “off” from math, that leaves an average of two to three hours per day on the remaining six days.

That average is important to observe, since human brains tend to learn best in short-ish shifts; some experts advise 20 minutes, but in my experience, as your mathematical studies advance, it can feel like you’re barely digging in after such a short time! You might find longer sessions to be more productive for you. Just try to avoid making them too long. There’s often a limit to how much new material you can cram into your head at once; your brain needs some time to “digest” new ideas, so taking a break after a while to do other things—or even better, get some sleep—can help you form better memories and connections. So, don’t wait until the last minute to start grinding out an assignment, leaving yourself with too little wiggle room: start homework as soon as you can, work on it frequently, and take breaks often.

Most of the advice above can help you regardless of whether you’re having trouble and struggling to improve, or if you’re already doing well and want to do even better. If you find yourself in that latter group, you may be seeking ways to further hone your skills and stretch your abilities. I suggest first reading all of the exercises in the textbook and attempting any you find interesting, even if not on the “suggested” list. Also take a look at the projects following some sections, and the “Problems Plus” sets after each chapter.
